From the analysis of Problem 3, I learned the role that material selection and composite layering play in high-temperature engineering, specifically regarding how Thermal Barrier Coatings (TBC) change the operating temperatures a system can endure. In the first condition, exposing the superalloy directly to hot gases resulted in a surface temperature of approximately 1236 K , which exceeded the 1200 K material safety limit. However, by applying a 0.5 mm ceramic TBC and accounting for thermal resistance, the internal superalloy temperature was reduced to 1104 K , bringing the component within a safe operating range. This highlighted that even a thin layer of material with low thermal conductivity (1 W/m·K) can provide substantial protection through its high thermal resistance.

These concepts are incredibly useful for aerospace engineering, particularly in the design of propulsion systems or airframes that experience extreme thermal loads. Understanding how to construct and solve thermal resistance networks allows for the prediction of failure points before a prototype is even built. This is a fundamental step to understanding how more complex systems can be built for maximum cooling performance.
